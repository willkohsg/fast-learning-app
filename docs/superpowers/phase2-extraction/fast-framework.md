## FAST Pillar Definitions

### F – Facts, Figures & Formulas

**One-Line Definition (William's words):**
> "Build the foundation. Increase recall speed. Remove hesitation." (p. 345)

**Full Definition:**
> "the foundation of instant, confident recall" (p. 17)

**What It Fixes:**
- Instant recall – no hesitation, no guessing (p. 261)

**Core Concept (The Distinction):**
> "Imagine I show you a song you've heard a hundred times. You recognise it immediately. But if I ask you to sing it from scratch – can you? That's Arjun and his formulas. He recognises them when he sees them. But in an exam, nobody shows them to him. He has to produce them from nothing. And he's never practised that." (p. 348)

The book emphasizes the critical difference:
> "Reading feels like learning. Recalling is learning. When you read a formula, your brain recognises it. When you recall a formula from memory with the notes closed, your brain owns it." (p. 97)

**The 3R System (William's Method):**
1. **Retrieve** – actively recall the formula without looking
2. **Reinforce** – write it down 3 times to build muscle memory
3. **Repeat** – space out recall: Day 1 → Day 3 → Day 7 → Day 14 (p. 378-381)

**Symptoms of F-Pillar Weakness:**
- Forgets formulas during exams
- Unsure about basic concepts
- Slow to recall
- Cannot answer simple questions under pressure
- Reading notes feels like studying but isn't

**Page References:** 17, 261, 345-390, 97

### A – Analysis of Questions

**One-Line Definition (William's words):**
> "Decode the question. Understand the demand. Eliminate confusion." (p. 391)

**Full Definition:**
> "the skill of decoding what an examiner is actually asking" (p. 17)

**What It Fixes:**
- Precision – understand exactly what is asked (p. 269)

**Core Concept:**
> "There's a very big difference between reading and decoding." (p. 169)

The book emphasizes:
> "When I mark exam papers, I'm not seeing students who don't know the topic. I'm seeing students who understood the question wrongly. They chose the right formula but applied it to the wrong situation. They had the knowledge – they just didn't know how to read the question." (p. 55)

**Real Example – The "Hence" Story:**
Hui Ling lost 17 marks because she missed the word "hence" repeated in the question. It meant "use your previous answer as the stepping stone for this part" but she treated it as decoration. (p. 131-136)

**The QTM Method (William's System):**
1. **Question** – break the question into parts, identify keywords ("hence", "find", "express", "show that")
2. **Topic** – match it to the correct topic, draw from your mental library
3. **Method** – choose the fastest solving method, avoid unnecessary steps (p. 428-434)

**Symptoms of A-Pillar Weakness:**
- Misreads or misunderstands questions
- Doesn't know what is being asked
- Confused when questions look different
- Struggles with word problems
- Jumps straight into working without reading fully
- Guesses the meaning and misses hidden conditions
- Feels overwhelmed by long or wordy questions

**Page References:** 17, 269, 391-442, 55, 131-136, 169

### S – Solving Patterns

**One-Line Definition (William's words):**
> "Know exactly how to start. Follow a proven solving flow. Get marks predictably." (p. 445)

**Full Definition:**
> "a library of proven starting points for every question type" (p. 17)

**What It Fixes:**
- Efficiency – correct starting point every time (p. 273-280)

**Core Concept:**
> "Before FAST, Arjun's working looked like he was wrestling with the question on the page. After FAST, it looked like he was having a conversation with it." (p. 448)

The book emphasizes:
> "Most students do not have a Solving Pattern Library – a set of predictable approaches for every question type. Top students do. Weak students don't." (p. 142)

And:
> "It's the difference between a chef who improvises every dish from whatever is in the kitchen – and a chef who has a repertoire of mastered recipes he can execute perfectly every time." (p. 448)

**The Pattern Before Process System:**
1. **Pattern** – identify the question type and recognise which category it belongs to
2. **Process** – apply the correct solving flow for that pattern
3. **Presentation** – show neat, logical working the examiner can follow (p. 477-481)

**Metaphor:** 
> "You stop reinventing the wheel. You just drive." (p. 663)

**Symptoms of S-Pillar Weakness:**
- Doesn't know how to start
- Tries luck and guesses the solving method
- Writes messy, random steps hoping something works
- Blanks out when questions look unfamiliar
- Cannot solve efficiently
- Messy or incomplete working

**Page References:** 17, 273-280, 445-487, 142, 448, 663

### T – Time Management

**One-Line Definition (William's words):**
> "Master the clock. Prevent panic. Finish the paper with confidence." (p. 489)

**Full Definition:**
> "the system for finishing papers with control, not panic" (p. 17)

**What It Fixes:**
- Control – never overspend, always finish (p. 285)

**Core Concept:**
The core rule William teaches:
> "No question receives more time than its marks justify. Move on. Return with fresh eyes." (p. 492)

> "Distance creates clarity. Pressure creates paralysis." (p. 492)

**Real Example – The 23-Minute Mistake:**
Arjun spent 23 minutes on a 4-mark question, which caused him to abandon 18 marks' worth of questions at the back of the paper that he actually knew how to solve. (p. 491-492)

**The IQST Timing Algorithm:**
1. **I – Information** – scan the question quickly
2. **Q – Question Requirement** – identify exactly what is asked
3. **S – Solving Pattern** – choose the correct method
4. **T – Time** – allocate appropriate time; move on if budget runs out (p. 523-527)

**Skip-and-Return Strategy:**
The book emphasizes:
> "No question gets more than its allocated time. Move on. Return later with fresh eyes." (p. 149)

**Symptoms of T-Pillar Weakness:**
- Never finishes on time
- Spends 15+ minutes on Question 1 without realising it
- Gets stuck and freezes – doesn't know when to move on
- Leaves large sections unattempted at the back of the paper
- Poor exam rhythm
- Panic responses to time pressure
- Rushes and makes careless mistakes

**Page References:** 17, 285, 489-535, 149, 492

## Error Category → Pillar Reinforcement Mapping

William does not explicitly use the standard error categories (Knowledge gap, Misapplied rule, Confusion between concepts, etc.) in his book. However, his framework reinforces these indirectly:

| Conceptual Error Category | FAST Pillar That Reinforces It | William's Connection | Clarity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Knowledge Gap** (student doesn't have the formula/concept) | **F** – Facts & Formulas | The 3R recall system directly addresses gaps in formula recall: "By week four: all eleven – in under ninety seconds" (p. 348) | CLEAR |
| **Misapplied Rule** (student has knowledge but uses it wrongly) | **A** – Analysis + **S** – Solving Patterns | QTM decoding + pattern recognition prevents misapplication: "He chose the right formula but applied it to the wrong situation" (p. 55) is fixed by A pillar | CLEAR |
| **Confusion Between Similar Concepts** (e.g., mistaking indices for equations) | **S** – Solving Patterns | The S pillar pattern library resolves this: "It looked like simultaneous equations but it was actually indices in disguise. I caught it." (p. 936) | CLEAR |
| **Poor Exam Strategy** (e.g., spending too long on one Q) | **T** – Time Management | IQST algorithm directly targets this: "No question gets more than its allocated time" (p. 149) | CLEAR |
| **Anxiety/Panic Response** | **A** + **T** combined (primarily) | Emotional stability component: "Breathing reset – four counts in, hold four, out four – before opening any exam paper. Twelve seconds. Measurably reduces anxiety." (p. 929) | CLEAR |
| **Lack of Practice** | **S** + **F** combined | Mistake journaling (all pillars) + consistent daily routine: "Ten minutes daily is not less than three hours weekly. For skill-building, it is more." (p. 574) | CLEAR |

**William's Own Error Logging Framework (p. 819-821):**

The mistake journal method he prescribes:
1. Write the question or brief summary
2. Write what you did wrong and why
3. **Identify which FAST pillar failed: F, A, S, or T**
4. Write the correct approach in your own words
5. Write: "Next time I will..."

This explicitly connects errors to pillars for self-diagnosis.

## Voice Pattern Library

### Rhetorical Moves & Distinctive Phrases

#### 1. **The Contrast Move** (Juxtaposing before/after, weak vs. strong)
- "Knowing means your child can explain the concept at the dining table... Scoring means your child can perform – under time pressure, with an unfamiliar question, in an exam hall" (p. 40)
- "Same content knowledge. Completely different results." (p. 49)
- "It looks like he's having an argument with the question on the page... Now it looks like he's having a conversation with the question. And winning." (p. 146)
- "Not because he worked harder. Because he finally worked in a way that worked." (p. 129)

#### 2. **The Metaphor Move** (Vehicles for difficult concepts)
- **GPS metaphor:** "Think of FAST as a GPS system for exams. A GPS doesn't make you a better driver overnight. But it gives you a reliable route" (p. 186)
- **Plant metaphor:** "You cannot grow a plant in poor soil and blame the seed. Change the environment, and the student changes with it." (p. 600)
- **Bucket with a hole:** "It was like trying to fill a leaking bucket by pouring water in faster." (p. 128)
- **Fire drill metaphor:** "If a building has never rehearsed its evacuation route, even sensible adults panic when the alarm goes off" (p. 87)
- **Chef metaphor:** "It's the difference between a chef who improvises every dish from whatever is in the kitchen – and a chef who has a repertoire of mastered recipes he can execute perfectly every time." (p. 448)
- **Singing metaphor:** "You recognise it immediately. But if I ask you to sing it from scratch – can you? That's Arjun and his formulas." (p. 348)

#### 3. **The Question Move** (Asking rhetorical or diagnostic questions)
- "What is this question asking? Which topic does this belong to? What pattern does this match? Which method is fastest? How many marks is this worth? How much time should I spend here?" (p. 162)
- "Did you review your papers after getting them back?" (p. 582)
- "Why didn't anyone teach him this before?" (p. 539)

#### 4. **The Quiet Moment Move** (Narrative silence, understated emotional weight)
- "Mrs Pillai stood in the doorway for a moment. Then she went quietly to the kitchen and made Priya a cup of Milo. She didn't say anything when she brought it in. She just set the cup down beside her daughter and left the room. Some moments don't need words." (p. 748)
- "She stood quietly for a moment, listening. Then she stepped back and waited. There was no rush. She had all the time in the world." (p. 942)

#### 5. **The One-Sentence Truth Move** (Distilling complex ideas into single powerful statements)
- "The difference between Sofia and a top scorer isn't knowledge. It's what happens at each stage of this cycle." (p. 249)
- "A hurdle you can name is a hurdle you can clear." (p. 153)
- "The struggle is not a sign that FAST isn't working. The struggle is the work." (p. 234)
- "Distance creates clarity. Pressure creates paralysis." (p. 492)
- "A weakness you can see is a weakness you can fix. A weakness you cannot see is a trap waiting to spring." (p. 137)
- "Not because he was more intelligent. But because Amir has, without even realising it, developed a performance system." (p. 50)
- "So this is what it feels like. Not brilliant. Not invincible. Just – ready." (p. 959)

#### 6. **The Diagnosis Move** (Naming the real problem, not the surface one)
- "The problem isn't ability. It's architecture." (p. 82)
- "The problem wasn't the system. It was him." (p. 569)
- "The problem wasn't a lack of effort. It was the wrong order." (p. 76)
- "His problem may not be what you think it is. It is rarely a lack of effort. It is rarely a lack of intelligence. It is almost always a missing system for how to think inside an exam." (p. 55)

#### 7. **The Reframe Move** (Changing how students interpret struggle)
- "This 'slowness' is necessary... This is not a problem. This is progress." (p. 213)
- "That voice is not giving you facts. It is giving you feelings dressed as facts. There is a difference." (p. 922)
- "The plateau isn't a ceiling. It's a signal." (p. 905)
- "The plateau had not been a ceiling. It had been a doorway – and the workflow was the key that opened it." (p. 906)

## Voice Pattern Summary

**William Koh's voice is:**
- **Calm and grounded** (not hyped; acknowledges struggle is real)
- **Highly narrative** (tells stories more than lists facts)
- **Diagnostic rather than prescriptive** (identifies the real problem first)
- **Emotional but not sentimental** (care embedded, not on the surface)
- **Systematic but human** (framework is rigid, application is flexible)
- **Repeating key truths** (distance creates clarity, structure reduces anxiety, identity shapes performance)
- **Singapore-specific** (family-centered, exam-obsessed, tuition-aware)
- **Empathetic to parents and students** (addresses both equally; validates struggle)

## Open Questions for William

1. **Error Category Precision:** In Chapter 9, the diagnostic table lists error types and their pillar mappings. Are these the ONLY error categories you track with students, or are there others (e.g., careless errors, incomplete working, arithmetic mistakes) that don't map cleanly to F-A-S-T?

2. **T-Pillar vs. Anxiety:** You mention emotional stability (Component 4, p. 584-590) and breathing resets as part of the system. Is anxiety management its own 5th pillar, or is it handled within T and A? The book groups it under "Critical Components" rather than core FAST.

3. **F-Pillar Depth:** The 3R system focuses on formula recall. But does the F pillar also include:
   - Concept understanding (not just formulas)?
   - Unit knowledge / dimensional analysis?
   - Basic arithmetic fluency?
   Or is F strictly about instant formula recall?

4. **S-Pillar Patterns:** You mention "a library of proven starting points for every question type." Do you have a master list of how many patterns exist per secondary maths topic (e.g., Quadratic Equations has 3 core patterns – factorise, complete square, formula)? Or is pattern discovery student-specific?

5. **Error Logging Fidelity:** In the mistake journal, students write "which FAST pillar failed." How do you handle errors that genuinely involve TWO pillars (e.g., misread question + ran out of time)? Do you have a hierarchy (primary vs. secondary pillar)?

6. **Integration Timing:** The weekly workflow has an "Integration" day (Friday) combining F+A+S+T. Why Friday specifically? And at what point in a student's FAST journey do they start integration drills (week 1, week 4, after mastery of individual pillars)?

7. **Transferability:** You mention FAST as a "life skill" (p. 940-941) and reference university assignments and job applications. Have you developed specific guidance on how students transfer FAST decoding/pattern-recognition to non-maths domains, or is it intuitive once they internalise the framework?

8. **Careless Errors:** The book doesn't explicitly categorise "careless errors" (e.g., arithmetic slip, sign error, unit error). Do these belong to F, T (rushing), or a separate category? Or does the S-pillar emphasis on "clean, logical working" naturally prevent them?

## Summary for Diagnostic Tool Development

**What the diagnostic tool must capture:**

1. **The Error-to-Pillar Mapping is CLEAR and authoritative:**
   - Forgot formula → F
   - Misread question → A
   - Chose wrong method → S
   - Ran out of time → T
   - Messy working → S
   - Panic/overthinking → A+T

2. **The Pillars Have Distinct Methods:**
   - F uses 3R (Retrieve, Reinforce, Repeat)
   - A uses QTM (Question, Topic, Method)
   - S uses Pattern Before Process
   - T uses IQST (Information, Question, Solving, Time)

3. **The Framework is Narrative + Systematic:**
   - Always contextualize errors in student stories
   - Always show the before/after transformation
   - Always name what the real problem is, not the surface symptom

4. **Identity and Emotional Safety Matter:**
   - The tool should help students move from "I'm bad at maths" to "I need to fix my A pillar"
   - Celebrate process, not just results
   - Normalize plateau as a doorway, not a ceiling
